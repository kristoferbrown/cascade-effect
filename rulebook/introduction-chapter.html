Basics

Cascade Effect is a role-playing game where the players create and control unique characters that must struggle to find their way and leave their mark in a world full of mysteries and adventures. The game is played in a small group; one person in the group is the game master (GM), and everyone else in the group plays a character. It is the game master's job to tell the story and control any friends and enemies that the player's characters meet. The GM will keep the game moving along, arbitrate all the game's rules, and try to tell a story that challenges all of the characters and gives each character some time in the spotlight. The first time you play, every player other than the GM will create unique characters that they can play as in future game sessions.

The World

Cascade Effect takes place in a world that is much like our own, but it is exaggerated with more drama, polarized conflict, and risks of danger. In this world mysteries and crimes are more likely to remain unsolved, violence is more likely to occur and more likely to go ignored, conspiracies are more likely to be real, and the average person isn't as sure that the strange rumor they heard or that creepy video they saw were just hoaxes. One thing is certain though, every mystery has a perfectly rational scientific explanation, but only if you can find it.

It's the very near future (although the Setting section details alternative options for playing in the 1960s, the 1990s, the 2050s, and a few other time periods). Other than marginal improvements to consumer electronics and the rapidly dropping cost of solar power, technology hasn't had time to advance much. However, socially the world has become much more polarized. Politics have become strictly black and white, inequality has gotten much worse, and these conflicts are only accelerating. As tensions rise, many rebellious groups from every part of the political and social spectrum have risen up. Massive protests have become commonplace, and groups advocating secession or revolution are growing popular and organized. In even the most stable countries the threat of civil war looms on the horizon and perceptions of societal precarity are practically ubiquitous.

Recent Events

Global politics have become defined by the conflict between xenophobic nationalists and overoptimistic revolutionaries. The voice of the moderate is practically unheard.

Many countries are struggling with the consequences of recently enacted and widely protested radio frequency identification programs. This conflict is felt most acutely in the United States and China were the growing Postnational Frontier movement has many people opting out of citizenship entirely.

The Eurozone is being torn apart by failing economies and disagreements over austerity measures, social safety nets, and a growing refugee crisis. The recent rise in intense nationalism and xenophobia has made the list of countries considering exiting the European Union grow longer. Accelerationist organizations, such as the growing Arrondissement Fantôme, that think the only way to build a better society is to first destroy the existing one are very active in western Europe despite often being designated as domestic terrorists.

The Arab world is erupting into a second Arab Spring. The #NSS movement is making sudden and historically surprising progress in its goal to separate Western Sahara from the kingdom of Morocco and rebuild it as New Secular Sahara, the first nation to be fueled entirely by solar and desalinization. Conversely, the Middle East is in the process of trying to overthrow despots and elected leaders alike in the conflict between western ideals and religious fundamentalism.

Russia is collapsing under the weight of sanctions, increasingly active civil rights movements, and increased military expenditures. South America and Sub-Saharan Africa both have been witness to uprisings and civil wars that some believe may have been proxy wars hinting at the start of a new cold war.

Media Polarity

At the turn of the century, the democratization and diffusion of access to the means to create mass media was expected to bring about a new age of transparency and a blossoming of journalism and the arts. Unfortunately, two decades later it has had almost the exact opposite effect. The unintended consequence of the long tail of society claiming the reigns of the media is that all media outlets have had to become increasingly partisan in order to stay competitive. This trend is only exacerbated by the popularization of the emerging media platform known as The Truth. This increasingly popular platform allows anyone to produce their own high quality media outlet and content with the same ease as updating one's social media accounts. Because one can simply create the evidence needed to support one's views, the most radical voices have become the loudest voices.

Content is now so biased that it has become extremely difficult for even the most informed and educated people to tell fact from fiction. Disinformation, conspiracy theories, pseudoscience, and hoaxes run rampant. This issue is compounded by the fact that in the world of Cascade Effect, these ideas are much more likely to be to reflect reality.

National RFID Programs

Citing fears of foreign actors and domestic terrorism, several nations, including the United States, Great Britain, France, China, and Japan, have enacted long-range radio-frequency identification programs. The specifics of these programs vary by country, but in general they work by requiring all citizens to carry RFID cards at all times. These cards can be quickly scanned by handheld devices from a distance of up to 20 meters, allowing law enforcement officials to easily know if someone is carrying their ID, who they are, a list of recent locations that ID has been scanned at, and any information available in federal databases about that person.

The passage and enforcement of each of these programs have proven to be very difficult, particularly in the United States and China where they have been met with widespread dissent and protest. Every RFID program has faced both immense political opposition, often including violent protest, and large economic challenges. Due to these difficulties, mandatory RFID laws only just starting to be enforced in the largest cities, turning them into panopticons of surveillance. Many people that originally opposed and protested the programs, especially those with strong leftist or libertarian views have moved to more rural areas to avoid this.

The Postnational Frontier

In light of all of the recent polarization in the world, many new organizations and movements have been created. The movement with the most traction is the Postnational Frontier, which grew rapidly in response to the growing panopticon that national RFID programs created in large cities and the precipitously dropping cost of solar panels. This movement advocates opting out of citizenship, often by the melodramatic act of burning one's RFID card, and then moving to a rural area or a "Frontier Settlement". The first group to create a frontier settlement, the anarcho-socialist New Frontier is source of the name Postnational Frontier movement as a whole.

The PF describes four different types of community in the world: Federal Cities, Liberal Cities, Liberated Cities, and Frontier Settlements. This terminology has become commonplace in mainstream culture as well.

Federal Cities

A federal city is any city that has a currently enforced RFID program. Most cities that are inside a country with an RFID program and part of a metropolitian area with a population greater than one million are federal cities. Living in one of these cities requires carrying your RFID card at all times or risk arrest. This has resulted in populations in these cities skewing somewhat more towards conservative values than they previously had. New York City, Tokyo, and Beijing, and London were the first enforced federal cities.

Liberal Cities

Any city with no enforced RFID program is referred to as a liberal city. Most smaller cities inside countries with RFID programs and all cities in countries without these programs fall under this category. It's mostly business as usual in these cities and most of them have not changed culturally at all recently. While the vast majority of communities are currently in this category, many in the Postnational Frontier think liberal cities are only a temporary state before all nations have ID programs and can afford to enforce them in all communities.

Frontier Settlements

This category describes any settlement that has been recently created by an anti-RFID organization. These cities have only recently been made possible by the advent of cheap solar panels and semi-automated distributed logistics algorithms. Most of these settlements resemble a cross between Burning Man and a ramshackle solar plant than a traditional city.

The anarcho-socialist New Frontier has four of these settlements across western North America and one in Inner Mongolia, each of which operating under a from each according to their means and to each according to their need principle. The libertarian group The Wolf Tribe organization has several settlements across the US, each backed by a wealthy founder's stockpile of resources and operating under an anything goes philosophy. The #NewSecularSahara movement described itself as the first frontier country when it declared independence from Mororcco. The origin of the multi-billion dollar solar and desalinization infrastructure that seemed to appear overnight and made this declaration possible is an ongoing mystery.

Liberated Cities

Any city that has been "saved" from RFID programs is referred to as a liberated city. This term was originally coined when protesters in Olympia, Washington declared their city free from the Seattle's enforced RFID district. Enforcing ID laws became briefly impossible without deploying the US military to this city to kill US civilians, a seemingly politically untenable idea. However, since then Olympia eventually became a federal city and this term has more often been applied to cities that have suffered some sort of disaster or economic collapse that made enforcing ID laws impossible.

Liberated cities include the post-industrial no-man's land that Flint became, the flooded wasteland that climate change and recurring disasters turned New Orleans and Hong Kong into, and the war-torn ruins Aleppo, and the refugee overwhelmed Calais. Anything goes in a liberated city, they range from abandoned ghost towns to might-makes-right martial law zones.

What is the Yau?

The Yau is a realm of space that exists parallel to, but entirely separate from, normal space. Other than the blurry but unyielding shadows that stationary physical objects cast into the Yau, little interaction between these two realms is normally possible.

Very few people are aware of the Yau, and those that are aware have conflicting explanations and names for its existence and properties. The scientifically minded may call it infraspace, amplitudhedral depth, or sub-plank dimensionality, while those with a more experiential understanding may refer to it as the depths, the crisis mirror, or the daydream.

The name that finally stuck was based on the mistaken interpretation that turn-of-the-century era string theory's Calabi-Yau manifold was responsible for the Yau phenomenon. The only current scientific theory that predicts existence of the Yau, a developing theory of everything known as Orthodynamics, instead proposes that the Yau phenomenon arises from the nested three-fold symmetry of spatial dimensions and fundamental particles.

Under normal circumstance matter in physical space cannot detect or interact with anything in the Yau. However, certain exotic processes do allow small amounts information to pass between them, and over the eons evolution has found uses for these processes. Most vertebrates possess a Yau body, an extra-corporeal computational organ that supplements and mirrors the brain. The Yau body of humans is particularly well developed and is theorized to be an important element of the algorithm of consciousness.

What is the cascade effect?

The cascade effect is the name for the collapse of information from one thermodynamically separate realm to another. This anti-entropic process creates one important side effect: free energy.

In rare cases, humans have been known to develop high degrees of Entanglement between their physical brain and their Yau body. This heightened connection creates enough bandwidth for a person to trigger the cascade effect at will and make use of the free energy that is released as a consequence. In this game, you play as a character that has recently discovered this ability and can now begin learning "Styles", the applied uses of the cascade effect that manifest as superhumann capabilities.

Characters

All humans have a tenuous connection to the world's parallel Yau space, but players create characters that are truly entangled with the Yau and have the ability to control this connection. Any self-sustaining communication between the Yau and normal space is called a Cascade Effect, and player characters have learned to utilize these effects to improve themselves beyond the limits of the human body.

Any specific applied usage of the Cascade Effect is referred to as a Style. Characters can learn Styles that improve their mental abilities by storing extra mental processing resources in the Yau. Styles can also allow characters to do incredible physical feats by utilizing the surplus of thermodynamic energy that is created as a side effect of transferring enough information from the Yau to locally reverse entropy. Some extremely difficult Styles allow characters to bend the laws of physics so far that they almost appear almost indistinguishable from magic.

Typically players create new characters that only very recently discovered their capability to learn Styles. In the beginning, these characters are no more capable and informed about the world's mysteries than a normal person, but as they become involved in adventures and intrigue they can quickly become superhuman.

Gameplay

When playing Cascade Effect the GM will describe the situation and then the players will say everything that their characters say and tell the group what their character intends to do. Usually, your character automatically does whatever you say. However, when your character is presented with a challenge that has a significant chance of failing with consequences, then dice are rolled to determine how well your character does. The GM will tell you the results of what your character attempted and will narrate any impact this action has on the group's surroundings and any further challenges they face.

Using The App

Cascade Effect is designed to be played either on pencil and paper or with the Cascade Effect app. Players using the app to keep track of their character and look up rules can gather around the same table and play with people that are using paper character sheets, dice, and a physical rulebook. The rules describe everything you need to play with pencil and paper, but the app automates a lot of the details.

If you are using the app, when the GM tells you to roll dice all you have to do is tap on the trait that the GM told you to roll and the app will roll it automatically. Tell everyone how the roll turned out and the GM will describe the results. In some situations the GM may ask you to roll a trait differently than normal, pressing and holding on that trait will bring up more options allowing you to do so. In the rare situation that you need to roll something that isn't covered by your in-app character sheet, you can also roll dice manually.

The app lets you browse the rulebook, create new characters, and view your existing character sheets. Each character sheet has five sections: Skills, where you can roll any non-combat skill; Combat, where you can roll any attack or defense, or create a new attack; Status, where you can track your character's current health; Styles, where you can view your current styles and buy new ones; and Gear, where you can view and add new equipment.

Using Dice

When your character is presented with a challenge you must roll a number of dice determined by your character's stats to see how well things turn out. The GM will tell you what stats to roll and then you will roll the appropriate number of six-sided dice, each die that comes up 4 or above adds a degree of success to the result, every die that comes up 3 or lower does not. Additionally, if you roll any 6's you may reroll one of them, keeping the success you got from rolling it and potentially rolling another. If the reroll comes up as 6 again, you can continue re-rolling it and getting more successes from it until it comes up as a different number. The target number of successes the GM assigns a roll is the number successes you need to get to do an adequate job. If you roll less it is a failure, but if you roll extra successes your character performs exceptionally well.

For example, let's say you roll 5 dice on an action that requires 4 successes and the dice come up 1, 3, 4, 6, and 6. You reroll one of the 6's and get a 5. and get This action will be a modest success because you rolled 4 successes (the 4, 6, 6, and the re-rolled 5).

The number of dice you roll is determined by your character's stats. Each roll has two parts, an attribute and a skill; the GM or your character sheet will tell you which ones to pick for each roll. Each point you have in the attribute contributes one die to the roll. Each point you have in the skill contributes an automatic success to the roll.

For example, your character is trying to climb something so the GM tells you to make an Athletics roll. For athletics the related attribute is Agility, so if your character had 3 points in Agility and 1 point in Athletics you would roll 3 dice and add 1 success to what you roll. If the dice come up 1, 3, and 5; that's 1 success, plus the 1 you get from the skill, for a total of 2 successes.

Every skill has an attribute that is usually associated with it, so if the GM just says to roll that skill, you would roll it with the typical attribute. However, in some situations the roll may pair a skill with a different attribute. If this happens the GM will tell you what attribute and skill combination to use.

Instructions on what traits to roll are written down in as Attribute + Skill. Any roll can also be indicated by 2 numbers, the number of dice to roll + the number of successes to add. For example, if a roll was written as 5+2 you would roll 5 dice and add 2 successes to whatever you rolled.

If a situation arises where your character is at such a disadvantage that you would roll 0 dice, instead roll a single die, but you must roll a 5 or a 6 for the dice to count as a success.

Combat

Sometimes, such as during a fight, the in-game action starts moving so fast that special rules are needed to keep track of every action. When a fight breaks out the game breaks down into rounds. When this happens everyone involved rolls initiative and takes turns according to the results of the initiative roll with the highest rolls going first. During a round faster characters may get multiple chances to act while slower characters might only go once. During combat, the GM will control all of the enemies and determine the results of everyone's actions.

In combat, the game can make use of a grid to allow everyone a tactical view of the action. Each character occupies a one-meter space on the grid and can move around to attack others and avoid attacks. If you are using a grid, each hex or square (the GM chooses which kind of grid is most appropriate for the situation, the rules are designed to work with either grid) represents a one meter space and the rulebook will describe how actions happen on the grid using either the term a space or a meter. Some GMs and players prefer not to use the grid and to keep the action in combat more abstract, in this case you can use the rules for actions and styles to give a rough picture of where things are, but you don't need to get bogged down in the details.
