Basics

Cascade Effect is a role playing game where the players create and control unique characters that must struggle to find their way in a hostile world full of secrets. The game is played in a small group; one person in the group is the game master (GM), everyone else in the group plays a character. It is the game master's job to tell the story and control the friends and enemies of the player's characters. The GM will keep the game moving along, arbitrate all the game's rules, and try to tell a story that challenges all of the characters. The first time you play the other players will create unique characters that they can play as in future game sessions.

Players control characters that can learn Styles. Styles are what allow the rare people who are capable of learning them to utilize what's known as the Cascade Effect in order to bend the laws of reality in ways that grant amazing supernatural powers. Styled people can transcend the limits their bodies impose on them in order to survive dangerous situations and leave their marks on the world.

Typically players create new characters that only very recently discovered their capability to learn styles. In the beginning these characters are no more capable and informed about the world's mysteries than a normal person, but as they become involved in adventures and intrigue they can quickly become superhuman.

The GM controls the tone of the game's story as they tell it. It can be anything from high-octane action with world shattering consequences to intimate interpersonal drama. The game's rules are designed to work best with action-adventure stories and to give player's many tactical options should combat occur, but they are flexible enough to work with any story.

Gameplay

When playing Cascade Effect the players say everything that their characters say and tell the group what their character does. Usually, your character automatically does whatever you say, however, when your character is presented with a challenge that has a significant chance of a failure with consequences dice are rolled to determine how well your character does. The GM will tell you the results of what your character attempted and will narrate what is going on in the group's surroundings and the challenges they face.

Using The App

Cascade Effect is designed to be played either on pencil and paper or with the Cascade Effect app. Players using the app to keep track of their character and look up rules can gather around the same table and play with people that are using paper character sheets, dice, and a physical rulebook. The rules describe everything you need to play with pencil and paper, but the app automates a lot of the details.

If you are using the app, when the GM tells you to roll dice all you have to do is tap on the trait that the GM told you to roll and the app will roll it automatically. Tell everyone how the roll turned out and the GM will describe the results. In some situations the GM may ask you to roll a trait differently than normal, pressing and holding on that trait will bring up more options allowing you to do so. In the rare situation that you need to roll something that isn't covered by your in-app character sheet, you can also roll dice manually.

The app lets you browse the rulebook, create new characters, and view your existing character sheets. Each character sheet has five sections: Skills, where you can roll any non-combat skill; Combat, where you can roll any attack or defense, or create a new attack; Status, where you can track your character's current health; Styles, where you can view your current styles and buy new ones; and Gear, where you can view and add new equipment.

Using Dice

When your character is presented with a challenge you must roll a number of dice determined by your character's stats to see how well things turn out. The GM will tell you what stats to roll and then you will roll the appropriate number of six-sided dice, each die that comes up 4 or above adds a degree of success to the result, every die that comes up 3 or lower does not. Additionally, if you roll any 6's you may reroll one of them, keeping the success you got from rolling it and potentially rolling another. If the reroll comes up as 6 again, you can continue re-rolling it and getting more successes from it until it comes up as a different number. The target number of successes the GM assigns a roll is the number successes you need to get to do an adequate job, if you roll less it is a failure, if you roll extra successes your character performs very well.

For example, let's say you roll 5 dice on an action that requires 4 successes and the dice come up 1, 3, 4, 6, and 6. You reroll one of the 6's and get a 5. and get This action will be a modest success because you rolled 4 successes (the 4, 6, 6, and the re-rolled 5).

The number of dice you roll is determined by your character's stats. Each roll has two parts, an attribute and a skill; the GM or your character sheet will tell you which ones to pick for each roll. Each point you have in the attribute contributes one die to the roll. Each point you have in the skill contributes an automatic success to the roll.

For example, your character is trying to climb something so the GM tells you to make an Athletics roll. For athletics the related attribute is Agility, so if your character had 3 points in Agility and 1 point in Athletics you would roll 3 dice and add 1 success to what you roll. If the dice come up 1, 3, and 6; that's 1 success, plus the 1 you get from the skill, for a total of 2 successes.

Every skill has an attribute that is usually associated with it, so if the GM just says to roll that skill, you would roll it with the typical attribute. However, in some situations the roll may pair a skill with a different attribute. If this happens the GM will tell you what attribute and skill combination to use.

Instructions on what traits to roll are written down in as Attribute + Skill. Any roll can also be indicated by 2 numbers, the number of dice to roll + the number of successes to add. For example, if a roll was written as 5+2 you would roll 5 dice and add 2 successes to whatever you rolled.

If a situation arises where your character is at such a disadvantage that you would roll 0 dice, instead roll a single die, but you must roll a 5 or a 6 for the dice to count as a success.

Combat

Sometimes, such as during a fight, the in-game action starts moving so fast that special rules are needed to keep every action in order. When a fight breaks out the game breaks down into rounds. When this happens everyone involved rolls initiative and takes turns according to the results of the initiative roll with the highest rolls going first. During a round faster characters may get multiple chances to act while slower characters might only go once. During combat the GM will control all of the enemies and determine the results of everyone's actions.

In combat, the game can make use of a grid to allow everyone a tactical view of the action. Each character occupies a one meter space on the grid and can move around to attack others and avoid attacks. If you are using a grid, each hex or square (the GM chooses which kind of grid is most appropriate for the situation, the rules are designed to work with either grid) represents a one meter space and the rulebook will describe how actions happen on the grid using either the term a space or a meter. Some GMs and players prefer not to use the grid and to keep action in combat more abstract, in this case you can use the rules for actions and styles to give a rough picture of where things are, but you don't need to get bogged down in the details.
