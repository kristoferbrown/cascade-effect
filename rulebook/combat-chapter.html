Rounds & Initiative

When a fight starts, game time is measured in rounds. Each round represents a few seconds of time and is divided into individual turns. During each turn, every character involved in combat may act once. After everyone has acted once and deducted the speed cost of their action, the turn ends and the round continues with another turn. Any character that still has speed remaining may make an action during the new turn. Turns continue this way until everyone involved in combat either runs out of speed or opts not to take an action. Once this happens the round ends and a new round begins.

The order everyone acts in is determined by the results of a Wits + Initiative roll that happens at the start of combat. During each turn all characters act in the order of their initiative rolls, with the highest roll going first. Characters with the same number act according to who has a higher current speed total when initiative is rolled, characters still tie compare Initiative skill scores, then Wits scores, then Agility scores until the tie is broken.

When it is your time in the initiative order you can use a single combat action or any number of move actions. If it is your first turn in the round you can instead choose to do a full action, start an extended action, or continue an extended action. On your turn declare what action you want to do, deduct its speed cost, and then perform any rolls the action requires. After rolling, the action resolves and it is the next character in the initiative order's time to act.

Speed & Endurance

Speed is a measure of how much you can do in a round. You start each fight with a number of speed points equal to your speed score. Every action you take has a speed cost, deduct this cost from your current speed when you do the action. When your current speed is less than the cost of the action, you cannot do the action.

On your first turn in a round, you can do an action that has a higher speed cost than your current speed. If you do so, spend all of your speed and treat this action as a full action.

At the start of each new round, regain a number of spent speed points equal to your current endurance. Your total speed score is the maximum that you can regenerate up to.

Your current endurance rating can be damaged by injuries in combat. If you start a round with 0 endurance you immediately fall unconscious.

Movement

Moving any distance across normal terrain is a move action. Each space moved expends a point of speed. You can do multiple move actions in one turn, such as moving, then jumping over something, then continuing to move. After doing a move action, you cannot do any other type of action on that turn.

Normal movement represents your character running around the battlefield. You can also sprint as a full action that expends all of your current speed and moves you in a straight line any number of spaces up to double your current speed.

Attacks

Basic attacks are combat actions that take up a turn. On your turn declare this action, deduct its speed cost, and then roll it out. The roll for the basic attack has 3 parts, the attack roll, the location roll, and the damage roll.

The attack roll is a basic attribute + skill roll (Finesse + Melee or Strength + Unarmed Combat for close combat or Perception + Firearms or Ballistics for ranged combat). If the result of this attack roll is equal to, or greater than, the target's passive defense then the attack hits.

For each extra success you get on an attack roll you can influence the result of the location roll to an adjacent location. See the Locations & Criticals section for details.

After the attack hits, roll a d6 to determine the location. Rolling 1-5 determines the location hit while a roll of a 6 is a critical hit.

After determining the location hit, roll damage. The damage roll is determined by the weapon used. See chapter 7, Equipment and Artifacts for weapon damage statistics.

Locations & Criticals

If an attack hits, the attacker will roll a die to determine where the it hits according to the following list.

  1. Legs
  2. Off Arm
  3. Main Arm
  4. Lower Body
  5. Upper Body
  6. Critical Hit!

Rolling a 6 scores a critical hit. On a critical hit reroll the die to determine where the critical hit lands, if you roll a 6 again keep rerolling until you roll a location between 1 and 5. Each time you rolled a 6 increases the critical level by 1.

Each extra success you got on the attack roll above what you needed to hit gives you an extra adjustment on the location roll. For each adjustment you can change the result of the location roll to an adjacent location. If you still have adjustments left after moving the location to the desired spot, increase the critical level by 1 for each remaining adjustment.

Deal an extra point of damage to the location hit for each level of critical you scored. Also, the target of a critical hit loses a point of Endurance for each level of critical you scored.

For example, if you got 6 successes to hit a target that required 2 successes to hit, you get 4 adjustments to the location roll. If you then rolled a 6 on the location roll, the critical hit level would increase to 1 and you would reroll the location. If the reroll came up 5 (Upper Body), you could use 2 of your adjustments to move the location to 3 (Main Arm), and then the remaining 2 adjustments would increase the critical level 2 times (taking it from 1 to 3). If the result of the damage roll was 3 damage adding 3 critical levels to it would result in 6 damage to the target's main arm.

Damage & Dying

The damage done by an attack is determined by the weapon used (See chapter 7 for weapon stats). Each success on the damage roll adds a point of damage to the location hit. If no successes are rolled a minimum of 1 damage is applied. If the total amount of damage a location has taken exceeds your Wound Max, then that location is disabled until medical care is received. If the total damage on a location is more than double your Wound Max, then that location is considered destroyed until medical care is received. The effects of having disabled or destroyed body parts are listed below.

Disabled Location Penalties:
  1. Legs: You can only move 1 space per round.
  2. Off Arm: You can't use or attack with this hand. You can still attack with your main arm. If both arms are disabled you cannot attack.
  3. Main Arm: You can't use or attack with this hand. If your main arm is disabled you can still attack with your off hand, however, while using your off hand all automatic successes on the attack roll are rolled as dice instead. If both arms are disabled you cannot attack.
  4. Lower Body: You are unconscious until medical care is received.
  5. Upper Body: You are unconscious until medical care is received.
Destroyed Location Penalties:
  1. Legs: You are unconscious until medical care is received.
  2. Off Arm: You are unconscious until medical care is received.
  3. Main Arm: You are unconscious until medical care is received.
  4. Lower Body: Death.
  5. Upper Body: Death.

Taking heavy damage reduces your ability to regain Speed by draining your Endurance. For each point of damage above your Wound Max that a location takes, decrease your current Endurance by 1. Also, every time you are the target of a critical hit, decrease your Endurance by 1 for each critical hit level.

If your current Endurance is ever 0 or lower at the start of a round, then you immediately fall unconscious. Any time you are unconscious and have at least one bodily location that is disabled or destroyed you will begin bleeding to death and lose a point of endurance each minute. If your current Endurance reaches the negative of its normal total your character dies. Receiving first aid can restore Endurance to its normal total.

Defenses

When you are the target of an attack you can choose whether you wish to use an action to attempt to dodge it or not.

Attempting to dodge is a defensive action that costs 3 speed and can be done any time you are targeted by an attack, even though it is not your turn. The attacker makes an attack roll and you make a dodge roll. If your dodge roll is equal to or better than the attack roll, then the attack misses.

Normally an attacker only needs to tie your passive defense to hit, but if you use a dodge action tied rolls result in a successful dodge.. For example, a passive defense of 1 only requires a single success to hit while a dodge roll result of 1 requires 2 successes to hit.

Your Stamina also provides you with another layer of defense against unarmed attacks. Whenever an unarmed attack hits you, roll your Stamina and deduct the number of successes you get from the total damage the unarmed attack would otherwise deal to you.

Healing & First aid

First aid can prevent downed characters from bleeding to death. First aid is an extended action with a speed cost of 6. Every 3 successes you get on a roll of Intelligence + Medicine causes the target's endurance to move 1 point closer to normal. Restoring at least a single point of Endurance this way prevents any further Endurance loss due to blood loss until any further damage on a disabled or destroyed location is taken. If you have lost consciousness due to a negative current Endurance, restoring enough Endurance to turn your current Endurance into a positive number will cause you to regain concsiousness at the start of the next round.

The first aid action can also patch up disabled or destroyed body parts. Make an extended first aid check against a target number of 6. Once the number of successes has been met the destroyed body part is treated as disabled or the disabled body part is treated as normal. This does not actually heal any points of damage, it just allows you to use a disabled body part without the penalty associated with disabled or destroyed status. If this restores the use of a location that was causing unconsciousness, the consciousness will be restored at the start of the next round. If this part is damaged again it goes back to being treated as disabled or destroyed.

In order to attempt first aid, you must be carrying the appropriate equipment and be adjacent to the target of the healing. The target must remain in an adjacent space and cannot take standard or defensive actions without interrupting the first aid attempt. You can use a combat action with a speed cost of 2 to roll Wits + Medicine in order to attempt to locate improvised first aid equipment such as cloth for bandages or sticks to use as splints. The number of successes the GM will require for this action to work depends on where you are and severity of the wounds. If you do manage to locate it you'll need to use move actions to move to the improvised supplies, free them from anything they may be attached to, and bring them back to the person in need of first aid.

The only way to actually heal damage points done is with time or Entanglement. Damage you have suffered in combat heals slowly on its own. Every week you automatically heal 1 point of damage on each bodily location. If you are receiving appropriate medical care the healing interval decreases from a week to a day. If you spend 1 point of Entanglement each interval during the healing process the amount you heal each interval doubles. Disabled or destroyed body parts do not begin to heal until medical care is received, with medical care they heal at a rate of 1 point per week. Characters with certain styles can heal wounds on themselves and others more rapidly.

Range & Grids

When playing out combat in Cascade Effect, using a grid to depict combat is encouraged, but not required. If you are not using a grid all rules that refer to specific spaces and distances are just treated as an approximate abstraction for the GM to determine what a character can and cannot do. If you are using a grid, either a hexagonal grid or a square grid is acceptable for tracking character movement and depicting how combat plays out. Use the grid shape that best fits the terrain that combat takes place in, squares are better for interior and urban scenes and hexagons are typically better suited for natural environments.

For ranged attacks, determining if the target is range using the same method whether your grid is square or hexagonal. Determine if a target is within range by tracing a straight line from any point in your space to any point in the target's space, each space this line crosses increases the range to the target by one. If this results in a greater number than the range statistic of the attack being used, then the out of range penalty applies (all automatic successes on the attack roll are replaced by dice).

On a square grid all spaces that share either a side or a corner with your space are considered adjacent. This means that you can attack or move diagonally for the same speed cost as attacking or moving horizontally and vertically. Things that effect areas, such as explosions, determine the radius of the effected area on a square grid by using the following method: a radius of 0 is a single space, a radius of 1 is all spaces that are in or adjacent (either sharing an edge or sharing a corner) to a radius of 0, a radius of 2 includes all spaces in and sharing an edge (but NOT a corner) with a radius of 1. All radii greater than 2 are calculated the same way as a radius of 2, by including all spaces that share a side but not a corner with the previous radius.

On a hexagonal grid adjacency and radius effects are determined more simply than on a square grid. All spaces that share a side with a space are adjacent to it. Any radius includes all of the spaces in the previous radius and all the spaces adjacent to them.

Special Combat Situations

Ambush

It is possible for combat rounds to start before everyone is aware of their opponents. If this happens the first round of combat is treated as a surprise round. During a surprise round only the characters that are aware of the fight situation can act. The character's that were caught off guard must wait until the next round before acting. Surprised characters cannot attempt to dodge attacks. Characters acting in a surprise round can only take 1 turn during the round, regardless of how high their speed is.

When attempting to ambush opponents each character that is near the opponents must roll Finesse + Stealth. The targets of the ambush will all roll Perception + Alertness, depending on the circumstances). Every character with an alertness roll better than the lowest stealth roll can act in the round.

Cover

Standing behind objects gives you a defensive advantage. First determine what bodily locations are protected by the cover. For example, a low barricade might only protect legs, a higher one might protect legs and lower body, looking around a corner protects the legs, lower body, and off arm. If an opponent attacks you and hits a protected location this attack does no damage. If the cover protects all of your body then you cannot even be targeted by the attack.

If you are behind partial cover, you can use a move action (with a speed cost of 1) to turn it into complete cover by ducking behind it. You remain protected fully by this cover until the next action you take. If you do nothing but take cover in this way for an entire round, it counts as resting and will let you regenerate twice as much speed as normal.

If you attack from cover, then you are exposing, at least, your upper body and main arm. They will remain out of cover until you take an action to get back into complete cover. You can fire blindly from cover to avoid exposing the upper body, but doing so imposes a 50% miss chance.

If you move a space during a defensive action and end up behind cover you can opt to be treated as behind complete cover until your next action.

Any cover depends on line-of-sight. A position that has cover from one opponent may be completely open from the point of view of another. Line-of-sight is determined by the GM's discretion.

Endurance Loss

Injuries are the most common way to experience Endurance loss. Each point of damage dealt to a location above your Wound Max lowers your current Endurance by a point due to blood loss. Endurance lost via injury does not come back without adequate first aid.

Certain attacks and hazards can lower Endurance without dealing damage. Endurance lost this way comes back at a rate of 1 point per round spent resting.

Starting a round with 0 endurance causes you to immediately fall unconscious. If your endurance is below 0 and you have any disabled or destroyed bodily locations, then you are considered to be bleeding to death and begin losing a point of Endurance each minute. If your endurance is below 0, but you do not have any disabled or destroyed bodily locations, you are not bleeding to death and regain a point of Endurance each round as if you were resting. If at the end of any round you are unconscious, but not bleeding to death, roll a number of dice equal to your current endurance, if you get at least one success you regain consciousness.

Flanking

When a target is surrounded by multiple adjacent opponents defending becomes more difficult. A target is considered flanked when at least 2 opponents are on opposite sides of its space. As long as the target remains in a flanked space it cannot make any active dodge attempts until it moves into a space that is not flanked. If the target does not leave the flanked space, it suffers all the consequences of being unable to use defensive actions, including being vulnerable to stealth attacks. It requires at least 2 attackers to flank a target, if there are more attackers the flanking penalty remains the same.

Helpless Opponents

When targeting a helpless opponent (unconscious, asleep, tied up, etc.) that is within range with a normal attack action, you do not need to roll. You may instead opt to automatically hit the target (hit location is determined normally), or you may opt to treat your normal attack as a full action to deliver a coup de grace to the target resulting in an automatic critical hit to a location of your choosing.

Prone Opponents

A character lying on the ground can only crawl 1 space in a turn and any action attempted requires an additional success to complete. Standing up again is a move action costing 2 speed. Any character rendered unconscious by any means is also considered to have been knocked prone.

Rough Terrain

Entering space of rough terrain requires the Traverse action instead of a basic move action. You cannot enter a space of rough terrain as part of the movement included in any action other than the Traverse action.

You cannot end a defensive action inside rough terrain, including any rough terrain you may already be standing on. As long as you remain in a space with rough terrain, you can only use defensive actions if there is adjacent normal terrain that you can move into as part of the defensive action. If there is no adjacent normal terrain, you cannot use defensive actions and suffer all related consequences, including being vulnerable to stealth attacks.

Stun

A stunned target cannot take any actions. Any attack or hazard capable of stunning a target will have a duration listed in its description. A stun with a duration of 1 turn will cause the target to miss their next turn in the initiative order but they can take further actions in the round. A duration of 1 round would prevent any further actions until the start of the next round. A stunned target is not considered resting and regenerates speed at their normal rate.

Yau Combat

Combat situations may arise in which one or more participants are in the Yau. Unless you have styles that state otherwise, exiting the Yau takes too long to reliably accomplish in combat situations and your ability to influence regular space is severely limited. If you do wish to exit the Yau as fast as possible, doing so requires an extended action with a roll of Willpower + Guts, an interval of a full round, and requires 6 success for each person that can currently see the space you are trying to exit the Yau into.

Acting in the Yau happens at a slower pace than in normal space. Any action you take is considered a full action with no Speed cost. As such you can only act on your first turn in the round, but you do not lose any Speed. Your ability to move is limited to a number of spaces equal to your Charisma score, but you can target your actions at anything within this number of spaces without moving.

If you are in the Yau without having brought your physical body with you (such as if you have less than 4 ranks of Sublimation, or if you are otherwise separated from your physical body), all rolls you make in the Yau (with the exception of trying to exit it) are made disadvantaged with automatic successes replaced with dice.

Extra-Yau Combat

When targeting those outside the Yau from within it, you can attack them as a full action by attempting to sever the connection between their Yau body and their physical body. To do so roll your Charisma + Guts and the target rolls their Willpower + Guts. If you beat the target's roll and they have no styles or unused style points, then they instantly fall unconscious for a number of minutes equal to the difference in rolls. If you beat the target's roll and they do have styles or unused style points, they instead lose a number of points of Entanglement equal to the difference between the two rolls. If the target is already at 0 Entanglement when the attack is attempted and you beat them by more than 3 successes, they instantly fall unconscious for a number of rounds equal to the difference in rolls.

Other than this attack, the only ways you have to influence those in physical space is by using the one of the Charisma-based styles, Induce Cascade, Pneumaplegia, or Telethesia.

Intra-Yau Combat

When fighting someone that is completely inside the Yau, you can attempt to lock them down by entering the same space and making resisted Willpower + Guts rolls. If you get more successes, neither of you may make any further actions or movements this round. On each subsequent round repeat this roll, the winner decides if the lockdown continues or if they instead move to an adjacent space and begin acting normally.

If you are rolling to lock down someone and you roll 3 successes more than you needed to win the roll, you may choose to force them to exit the Yau. If you do this to someone that is in the Yau separate from their physical body they will be removed from your space immediately and then wake up in their body, no matter how distant it is, at the start of the next round. If you do this to someone who has brought their physical body with them into the Yau, they immediately loose the ability to act in the Yau, become visible but intangible and unconscious at their current location in physical space at the start of the next round, tangible but unconscious at the start of the next round, and conscious and able to act normally at the start of the next round after that.
