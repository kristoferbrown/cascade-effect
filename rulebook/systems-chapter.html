Types of Rolls

Most of the time the actions you describe your character doing happen without an issue, however, when a task has a significant risk of failure you must roll dice to see how well your character does.

The most frequently used roll is the basic roll, a single roll where the results indicate the degree of success. This roll is used for the majority of non-combat situations, but in special circumstances the GM may decide to use a modified roll instead.

Basic Rolls

The basic roll is used for the majority of tasks. In a basic roll, the GM will tell you what skill to roll and set a target number of successes required on this roll. The GM determines the target number by considering the difficulty of the action and any circumstances that may help or hinder your character. If you roll the required number of successes the action goes well, if you don't it is a failure. The more successes you beat the target number by, the more successful the action is. Conversely, the fewer successes you roll, the greater the failure is. After you make the roll the GM will tell you the results of it.

The target number the GM sets for a basic roll is determined by the difficulty of the task and any circumstances that may help or hinder your character. A trivial check might only require only 1 success while something almost impossible would require as many as 6. Keep in mind that any check with a target number greater than 6 is impossible for a normal human to succeed at, so target numbers above 6 should be reserved for things that only a person with superhuman powers could succeed at. You can ask the GM how difficult a basic check would be, depending on the nature of the check the GM could tell you the exact number, a vague approximation, or nothing at all.

Skipping a Roll

In situations where your character is able to take their time with a task that isn't using an extended roll, you may be able to skip rolling entirely. If the target number of successes on the roll is equal to or greater than the number of points you have in the skill that would normally be rolled, you can skip the roll and automatically succeed. Skipping rolls this way is only available at the GM's discretion in low-stress scenarios where your character is able to take their time.

Modified Rolls

The majority of non-combat rolls are normal basic rolls, but in some situations a modified version of this roll may be more appropriate. The GM will decide if the current situation is better represented by one of the modified rolls and instruct the players on how to roll it.

The resisted roll is made when your character's efforts are in direct opposition to someone else and requires both you and the other person to make rolls to show who prevails.

The distributed roll is made when your character is attempting to accomplish multiple goals with a single roll.

The extended roll is used when your character is attempting something over a period of time and uses multiple rolls to represent your character's progress towards completing the task.

Resisted Rolls

Resisted rolls are only used when your action is being actively resisted by someone else.

These rolls act just like basic checks, but both you and your opponent make a roll and instead of aiming for a target number you are aiming to beat your opponent's roll. The GM will determine what skills you should roll. Common resisted rolls include your ability to lie vs. the opponent's ability to detect lies, your stealth vs. an opponent's alertness, your athletics skill vs. an opponent's athletics skill. The greater the difference between the results of the two rolls the greater one person's success over the other person is. In the event of a tie, the GM will decide who has the advantage and therefore succeeds.

Distributed Rolls

Distributed rolls are used to represent scenarios where your character is trying to accomplish multiple goals with at the same time. In a distributed roll, make a normal basic roll but then assign each success your roll scores to one of goals. The GM will assign a target number to each separate goal, and if the number of successes you assigned to that goal exceeds it's target, then you accomplish that goal. The GM will communicate how difficult each goal is based on how much information is available to your character. You may receive specific, vague, or no information about the relative difficulty of each goal.

For example, attempting to sneak into a secure location and steal an artifact could be represented with a distributed roll with two goals, remaining undetected and acquiring the artifact. Based on how you assigned the successes you got on a Stealth roll and how difficult each goal was, you may succeed at one goal and fail the other.

Extended Rolls

Extended rolls are used represent long term tasks in which it is important to determine the amount of time and effort the task takes to complete. These rolls aren't used to determine if your character succeeds at a task, but rather when your character completes the task.

These rolls are like a basic roll spread out over several rolls. For an extended action the GM will set a target number, typically a number that is higher than could be achieved by a basic roll, and then tell you what skill to roll and what the interval for the rolls will be. The interval is a period of time that the check is measured in, and the GM will decide if the check is best measured in turns, rounds, minutes, hours, days, or weeks. Once per interval you get to make a roll and add the results to the total number of successes you have rolled in previous interval rolls. Once the total number of successes equals the target number you have completed the check successfully. If you wish to increase the degree of your success and you have additional time you may take extra intervals to amass more successes than the target number.

Unlike basic actions where the target number is indicative of the difficulty of the check, the target number for an extended check is indicative of the amount of time and effort needed to complete the task. It is up to the GM to set a realistic target number and interval for the check. The GM will also determine how much attention you must pay to the extended check per interval to get a roll for that interval, some checks will require constant attention, while some longer checks you can walk away from for days and still get to make a roll for that interval.

The GM may also place other restrictions on an extended check. For example, some checks may only allow you a limited number of intervals to amass enough successes before you run out of time. Some extended checks allow you to take breaks from them and pick them up later while some will fail if you miss a single interval. Some difficult checks might require you to roll at least a certain number of successes per interval or you fail.

Extending a Basic Roll

In some situations when you fail a basic roll or are presented with a roll that you are not likely to make you can extend the roll. You can only extend a check in situations when you can take your time with the action, and only in situations where extra care and effort are helpful (for example, you can't jump further just by being careful). The GM will decide if it makes sense that an action could be extended, many physical or social actions you will only get one shot at, but other times being extra careful and taking extra time pays off. If you decide before a basic roll to extend it, just treat it as a normal extended roll instead. The number of intervals may be limited by the time you have available. If you decide to extend a basic roll after failing it, you can still treat it as an extended roll and add the few successes you did get on the basic roll to your total but, at the GM's discretion, the initial failure may result in a setback that makes the task more difficult.

Entanglement

Entanglement is a measure of the intensity and potential bandwidth of the connection between a person's physical brain and the portion of their consciousness that resides in an intangible structure called the Yau body. Every vertebrate animal has, at least, some Entanglement, although all animals and most people only have a small amount. Only people capable of learning styles have enough Entanglement for it to be measured in quantities greater than a single Entanglement point.

People with styles can use Entanglement to induce a Cascade Effect that allows them to use some of their powers and in doing so, a portion of the connection between their brain and Yau Body is earmarked for running, maintaining, and recovering from that power. Eventually, that portion of the connection will be freed up and available for active use again. This relationship results in the sensation that Entanglement is a resource that can be used, burned up, or expended until it slowly regenerates.

Using Entanglement

Several styles you may learn as you gain experience require Entanglement expenditure to activate. In addition, anyone can spend Entanglement on the following two abilities.

Technique

Any time that you are about to make a check you can spend a point of Entanglement to assist with a roll. Each point of Entanglement spent this way adds one automatic success to the roll.

Recover

When you are injured, you normally heal a point of damage once every set interval of time. You may spend a point of Entanglement during that interval to heal two points of damage instead.

Entanglement Balance

Your Entanglement score represents your natural Entanglement balance point. If you currently have less than this number, you will slowly recharge back up to that point. On the other hand, if you have gained enough Entanglement from the external sources to push you above this number you will slowly bleed off the excess until you are back at your natural balance point.

Once per day, typically while your character is sleeping, adjust your current Entanglement total 1 point closer to your Entanglement score.

Environmental Entanglement

Your connection to your Yau Body can be degraded or bolstered by external conditions. Large nearby changes in inertia, entropy, or potential energy can induce minor Cascade Events that briefly influence the directionality of the natural flow of information to and from nearby Yau Bodies. Because of this, being near dangerous, explosive, high risk, or high-speed events may cause you to suddenly gain a point of Entanglement that you could use later.

Additionally, people that make a sudden expenditure of massive amounts of Entanglement can also induce minor Cascade Events that may cause others nearby to gain a point. Some people with many styles can even become so powerful that their mere presence can influence the Entanglement of those nearby. The first time you are in the presence of someone that has more than 60 total style points and more than twice as many total style points as you do, you will gain a point of Entanglement.

Sometimes gaining a point of Entanglement may be your first clue that something major is happening near you.

Crisis Sickness

Your Entanglement score is the level of Entanglement where you feel most comfortable and operate at peak efficiency. A person well below that point experiences fatigue, depression, depersonalization, dissociative episodes, and general malaise. These symptoms worsen the further your current total drops below that point. If you are at 0, you must get nine uninterrupted hours of sleep before your normal Entanglement balancing restores a point.

Conversely, a person with a current Entanglement total well above their Entanglement score experiences excitability, mania, nervousness, restlessness, derealization, insomnia, paranoia, and tremors. These symptoms worsen the higher your current total gets. Having more than double your Entanglement score causes you to develop what is typically referred to as crisis sickness, a condition that does not go away until you have less than double your Entanglement score.

Having crisis sickness causes constant tremors and jitters that are severe enough to deduct 1 die from all of your rolls. Spending Entanglement will get you back to safe levels faster than waiting for it to naturally drop, however, for each point you use while in this state you are dealt a point of damage to a random location.

Hazards

Darkness

While not a direct hazard, darkness aids anyone attempting to use stealth and could result in surprise attacks. Increasing levels of darkness provide the following effects.

Deep Shadow

Add one die to all stealth rolls.

Nighttime Outdoors

Add one success to all stealth rolls attempted.

Complete Darkness

Equivalent to complete blindness. All attacks suffer a 50% miss chance. All stealth rolls get a bonus of 3 successes.

Endurance Loss

Some attacks and hazards cause Endurance loss instead of damage. Damage done from constriction or strangulation can potentially cause lost Endurance instead of damage.

Electricity

As the strength of the electrical source increases coming in contact with it deals more damage to more locations and can stun or knock the target unconscious. Contact with increasing electrical power has the following effects.

Residential Wiring

Deals 1+1 damage to one location. If in combat, target is unable to take any action on their next turn.

Distribution Line

Deals 2+2 damage to one location and all adjacent locations. Target is knocked prone in an adjacent space and unable to act until the next round.

Transmission Line

Deals 3+3 to all locations. Target is knocked prone in an adjacent space and is unconscious until 10 successes have been accumulated on an extended Willpower + Toughness roll with an interval of 1 round.

Exhaustion

Each interval you spend exerting yourself to a degree similar to one of the following exertion levels deducts all of you current speed. Each interval of continued exertion after the first deducts a point of endurance. Once your endurance reaches 0 from continued exertion you must succeed at a Willpower + Toughness roll with a difficulty that increases by 1 each interval in order to continue this level of exertion. Once you fail this roll you can now only use lower levels of exertion and the difficulty of the rolls to continue resets to 1. Once you fail the roll to continue the lowest exertion level, a forced walk, you can still continue this exertion, however each interval will inflict a point of damage to all locations.

While you are exerting yourself you do not regain speed or endurance. Each interval of rest after exertion regains one point of endurance. Once your endurance is back to its normal value you begin regaining speed normally.

The exhaustion rules are never triggered by actions in combat, only by long term sustained exertion. However, if combat starts during exertion you start the fight with 0 speed. Treat your exertion modified endurance total as your normal maximum endurance rating during the fight. For the duration of combat you regain speed from rest actions and rounds ending as normal. When the fight is over you return to 0 speed.

Adverse conditions including extreme temperatures, encumbrance or rough terrain can reduce the duration of the intervals by up to half.

Forced Walk

An interval of 3 hours is used for any sustained low levels of activity.

Jog

An interval of 10 minutes is used for any activity that gets you out of breath.

Sprint

An interval of 1 minute is used when you are at maximum exertion.

Falling

Any character can fall 2 meters without getting hurt. Any fall greater than this deals 1 point of damage to the legs for each additional meter fallen. You can reduce this damage by making an Agility + Athletics roll. Each success on this roll reduces the damage done by 1 point.

Fire

Contact with fire deals a set amount of damage immediately. Continued exposure does this same amount of damage at the start of each round. All body parts directly exposed to the fire are dealt this damage. When a fire does exposure damage it also has a 50% chance to catch all exposed locations on fire. Any body part that has caught on fire is dealt 1+1 damage, in addition to any damage continued exposure causes, at the start of each round. A body part on fire continues to receive 1+1 damage each round, even if removed from the fire, until it is extinguished. Attempting to extinguish a location that has caught fire is a full action that requires one success on a roll of Wits + Athletics.

Torch

Deals 1+1 damage. A torch used as a melee weapon does the damage of a light club but only causes fire exposure damage and risk of igniting a body part the first time it hits you in a round.

Bonfire

Deals 3+1 damage. As long as you don't end an action in it's space, passing quickly through a small controlled campfire could be done without facing exposure damage by getting a single success on an Agility + Athletics roll.

House Fire

Deals 6+1 damage. A particularly intense inferno might also do fire damage to everything in adjacent spaces.

Suffocation & Drowning

The sum of your Stamina + Toughness is equal to the number of minutes you can hold your breath, exceeding this limit results in unconsciousness. Each minute spent unconscious after this point drains a point of Endurance. At the end of the minute that reduces your Endurance to the negative of it's normal value, you will die from lack of oxygen.

Toxins & Drugs

The effects of drugs, poisons, venoms and toxins vary greatly. Some general drug archetypes and their typical effects are listed below. The potency level of a drug is up to the GM and is typically rated on a scale of 1-3. Many drugs may combine multiple types of effects listed below. Note that due to wide variety of possible drug effects the list below is mostly only a suggestion of the possible short term effects of a drug. Most drugs and toxins are controlled substances that cannot be easily acquired. The actual drug effects and availability are up to the GM's discretion and the player's ability to roleplay. Slow acting drugs, diseases, and infections or those used as plot devices might have any plausible effect the GM desires.

Amnesiac

Target must succeed at a Willpower+Toughness roll with a difficulty equal to the drug's potency level to remember events occurring during the drug's duration, which is typically on the order of an hour. Increased dosages increase duration but not potency. Amnesia causing agents rarely have potency levels greater than 1 and frequently also cause sedative/hypnotic effects.

Potency 1: Scopolamine, Benzodiazepines

Analgesic

Painkillers allow you to continue using disabled body parts normally. Each level of potency allows you to ignore 1 point of damage above the wound max of a body part for the purposes of determining if the body part is disabled. Note that damage done above the wound max causes endurance loss and bleeding at normal rates. Some analgesics only effect a single body part while others effect the whole body. More potent analgesics frequently also have sedative or other effects.

Potency 1: Nitrous Oxide (Local)
Potency 2: Vicodin (All Locations)
Potency 3: Morphine (All locations)

Hallucinogen

Targets must succeed at periodic Willpower+Toughness rolls with a difficulty equal to the drug's potency level in order to act normally. Success indicates that you can tell what is real and act normally, however without multiple extra successes your intoxication may still be apparent to others. Mild failure results in obviously erratic but not dangerous behavior. Complete failure could result in either complete incapacitation or taking an obviously dangerous action. In combat, rolls must be made each round in order to act normally. Complete failure could result in attacking or dodging a non-existent threat. Deliriants are a particularly dangerous type of hallucinogen that is more likely to cause aggressive behaviors and inability to tell friend from foe when failing a roll.

Potency 1: Phencyclidine (deliriant)
Potency 2: LSD, Psilocybin

Intoxicant

Drugs that cause impaired motor function and coordination reduce all attack, dodge, Athletics, and Drive rolls by 1 success per level of potency. Many analgesics and sedatives also have generic intoxication effects and vice versa.

Potency 1: Alcohol (drunk)
Potency 2: Alcohol (shit faced)

Irritant

Irritants are typically delivered in aerosol form and contact with skin causes intense pain. Exposure causes an immediate stun with a duration of a number of actions equal to the potency level. Each round of continued exposure starts with this same number of stunned actions. Irritants with a potency of 2 or greater also cause total blindness if they have any contact with the target's face. Effects of irritants typically have very short durations and begin to fade immediately after exposure stops.

Potency 1: Dilute capsaicin
Potency 2: Pure capsaicin, mace
Potency 3: Tear Gas

Necrotoxin

Necrotic venoms typically need to be injected via syringe or animal bite to have an effect. These toxins cause extreme pain and cell death at the injection site. Each level of potency does a 1+1 damage to the body location that was injected. Increased dosage can cause increased damage and spread to adjacent bodily locations.

Potency 2: Brown Recluse Venom
Potency 3: Rattlesnake Venom

Neurotoxin

Neurotoxins cause paralysis and death. For the duration of the toxin's effect the target is incapacitated and typically in great pain. Each interval (interval duration is determined by the GM) after exposure the target must succeed on a Stamina + Toughness roll with a difficulty equal to the potency level of the toxin. Failing this roll results in losing 1 point of Endurance per level of potency. If Endurance is reduced to the negative of it's normal value the target dies. Medical care is typically required to stop this process.

Potency 1: Heavy metals
Potency 2: Cobra venom
Potency 3: Aerosolized nerve agent

Sedative

During combat the target's maximum Speed and Initiative roll results are lowered by 1 for each point of the sedative's potency. Outside of combat the target must succeed on Willpower + Toughness rolls in order to remain awake.

Potency 1: Diphenhydramine
Potency 2: Barbituates, Benzodiazepines

Stimulant

Each level of potency increases the targets maximum Speed by 1 for the duration if the stimulant's effects. Potent stimulants often have side effects that could include jitters that remove 1 die from all attack rolls or a crash that acts like a sedative once the stimulant wears off.

Potency 1: Caffeine megadose
Potency 2: Amphetamine, Cocaine

Truth Serum

Target must succeed at a Willpower + Toughness roll with a difficulty equal to the drug's potency level to resist most non-dangerous suggestions during the drug's duration, which is typically on the order of an hour. Increased dosages increase duration but not potency. Amnesia causing agents rarely have potency levels greater than 1.

Potency 1: Sodium Thiopental, Amobarbatal
