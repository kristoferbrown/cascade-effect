Step 1: Character Concept

The first step of character creation is to come up with your character's concept. A character concept is a word or phrase that summarizes your character. One word archetypes or job titles like "cop" or "musician" are acceptable, but unique and descriptive titles will lead to a character that is far more interesting to play. A concept can be almost anything you can think of that fits in the game setting you are playing in.

You may wish to start thinking about a concept now and then move on to the rest of character creation, only to write a concept down at the end when you have a better idea of who you want your character to be. If you are using the app you will be given a second opportunity to edit your name and concept at the end of the creation process, so feel free to leave them blank the first time around. The top of the paper character sheets has a space to write a concept and your character's name.

Step 2: Select Attributes
Spend 12 Points on Attributes
Recommended 1 point minimum per attribute.

Attributes are the most fundamental aspects of a character and almost every character will have at least one point in every attribute. Each attribute is an innate characteristic of the mind or body. All die rolls include a single attribute. Each point in an attribute adds a die to rolls that include it.

During character creation, you can fill in 12 dots in the attributes section. At this point no attribute can go above 3. 3 points in an attribute is the absolute peak of a normal human's performance, 2 points is noticeably above average, 1 point is average, and 0 points in an attribute represents a significant disability. Later in the game, you can learn styles that improve attributes beyond 3 points. However, after doing so, whenever someone sees you use that attribute you are easily recognizable as superhuman.

It is highly recommended to put at least 1 dot in every attribute. An attribute with 0 points in it represents a significant disability. The GM may allow you to have 0 in an attribute if it fits your character concept in an interesting way and you plan on roleplaying the character's disability. If you are using the app a point is put into each attribute by default, but you can remove this point if you wish.

Offensive Attributes

Strength

Offensive
Unarmed

The ability to lift and move weight. Part of the damage roll in close combat. A 0 in this attribute means you can only lift 40 pounds, 1 means you can lift your body weight, 2 means you can lift up to double your body weight, and 3 means you can lift triple your body weight. If styles improve your Strength above 3, each additional point raises lifting power by an addition 600 pounds.

Finesse

Offensive
Melee

Coordination and manual dexterity. Part of the attack roll in close combat. A 0 in Finesse means that you are incredibly clumsy and whenever you gain a point of entanglement or are startled you automatically fail the next roll if it occurs within the next minute.

Perception

Offensive
Ranged

Sharp senses and good aim. Part of the attack roll in ranged combat. If you have a 0 in Perception choose a sense (either sight of hearing) at character creation that you are deficient in. All rolls for tasks that require this sense require 3 extra successes than they normally would.

Defensive Attributes

Stamina

Defensive
Unarmed

Ability to exert oneself and resist injury. Lets you take more damage in combat and regenerate Speed faster. A 0 in Stamina means that you are so frail that any time you take damage you are stunned for the rest of the round.

Agility

Defensive
Melee

Ability to move one's body quickly and nimbly. Part of the melee dodge roll and your character's maximum Speed. A 0 in Agility means you are so slow that each space you move in combat has twice its normal Speed cost.

Wits

Defensive
Ranged

The ability to think fast and under pressure. Part of ranged dodge and initiative rolls. A 0 in Wits causes the first round in any fight to be a surprise round at your expense.

Mental Attributes

Willpower

Mental
Extropy

Ability to resist coercion and temptation. Part of your Entanglement score, which allows you to use styles more often. A 0 in Willpower leaves you with an obsession, compulsion, or phobia that is so extreme enough to have severe social consequences.

Willpower styles can unlock Extropy, an ability that leads to immortality and access to arcane styles that allow the overtly supernatural abilities Abjuration, Evocation, and Sublimation.

Intelligence

Mental
Avatar

Ability to learn, comprehend and recall. A 0 in Intelligence leaves you with a significant learning impairment and an IQ below 50.

Intelligence styles can unlock Avatar, an ability that leads to immortality and access to arcane styles that allow you to manipulate the fundamental forces Chromodynamism, Electromagnetism, and Gravitonertia.

Charisma

Mental
Proxy

Improves social skills, likability, trustworthiness, and self-confidence. A 0 in Charisma leaves you with a strong social impairment that results in all rolls that involve the skill Charm or Manipulate (choose which at character creation, this choice remains permanent until your Charisma improves) require 3 successes more than usual.

Charisma styles can unlock Proxy, an ability that leads to immortality and access to the subtle but powerful arcane styles Pneumaplegia, Schizosomata, and Telethesia.

Step 3: Skills
Spend 6 Points on Skills

Skills represent training, practice, and expertise in a narrow field. Skills are rolled less frequently than attributes because they are more specific, however, when they are rolled they are more potent. While each point in an attribute adds a die to a roll, each point in a skill adds an automatic success to a roll.

At character creation, you have 6 points to spend on skills and no skill can go above 3. Unlike attributes, a 0 in a skill does not indicate a disability. A skill rated at 0 just means that you have no practical experience in that field above and beyond what it can be assumed an average person is familiar with. A 1 in a skill represents an amateur or hobbyist level of training. A 2 in a skill is sufficient to create a professional career around this skill. A 3 in a skill represents that maximum amount of mastery in a skill a normal human can have.

On the paper character sheets, there are two boxes next to each skill to write down what you roll when each skill comes up. Leave these boxes blank until after the next step (spending bonus points). If you are using the app these boxes calculated automatically and not shown in character creation.

Skills are listed on your character sheet sorted and colored according to what attribute they are typically rolled with. The last section of this chapter includes skill descriptions that also indicates what attribute each skill is typically rolled with. However, at the GM's discretion, certain circumstances may change the needed attribute. For example, the skill Drive may be rolled with Finesse when weaving through obstacles, with Wits when you have to suddenly brake for an unexpected hazard, or with intelligence to identify the model and capabilities of vehicles you encounter.

Optional: Custom Skills

There are blank skill slots on the sheet allowing you to create your own custom skills. Any specific field of training you may have that isn't covered by the default list of skills could be a candidate for a custom skill. Always consult with your GM when creating new skills.

Another use for custom skills is to create a skill that is similar to, but more specific than, one of the default skills. A more specific skill may still be applicable to the same situations as the original skill, but would grant a bonus to tasks specifically related to the custom skill. For example, the GM may allow you to create the custom skill Biochemistry, which would decrease the difficulty in biochemical tasks, but it could be used with increased difficulty in many situations that would normally require the skill Science.

Step 4: Spend Bonus Points
Spend 3 Points Anywhere

Fill in 3 more dots however you see fit divided between attributes and skills. Traits still cannot go above 3.

After you have spent all your points, you can fill in the two boxes next to the skills you chose. If you are using the app, these boxes are not shown in character creation and instead are calculated automatically.

The first box is how many dice you roll for this skill; this number is equal to the number of dots you filled for the attribute that is typically rolled with this skill. The correct attribute is indicated by the color of the skill or in the skill descriptions at the end of this chapter.

The second box is the number of automatic successes you add to this skill when it is rolled. At this point, the number of successes you add to a roll is equal to the number of dots you filled in on that skill.

The skills section of the character sheet is a useful place to write down things you roll frequently. You can write down the roll for any skill here for quick reference, even if you don't have any points in it.

Step 5: Details

In this step you will complete the other areas on the front of the character based on the choices you made in the previous steps.

If you use the app, this step is done automatically, so skip to last step.

Speed

This section is a meter that runs across the bottom of your sheet. The number of points of speed you have indicates how many things you can do in a round during combat.

In the box inside the Speed meter write down the sum of your Agility + every ODD point of Athletics + 3. For example, if you have 1 or 2 points in Athletics it adds 1 to your speed, but if you have 3 points in Athletics it adds 2 to your speed. This number indicates the amount of speed you start each fight with and also the maximum amount of speed you can have.

On the meter indicate the number you wrote down in the box by drawing a line after that many dots. Later, when you use speed you can use a paperclip or a light pencil mark to indicate your current number of speed points remaining.

Endurance

This section is a meter running just above the speed meter at the bottom of your sheet. Write down the sum of your Stamina + every EVEN point of Athletics + 3 in the box inside the Endurance meter. For example, if you have 0 or 1 points in Athletics it does not improve your endurance, but if you have 2 or 3 points in Athletics it improves endurance by 1. This number indicates how many speed points you regain at the end of a round in combat.

On the meter indicate the number you wrote down in the endurance box by drawing a line after that many dots. Later, your current total of endurance may drop below the number in the box. If this happens you can indicate it lightly with a pencil on the meter.

Entanglement

This section is a meter that runs across the bottom of your sheet. The number of points of Entanglement you have indicates how many times you can activate certain supernatural abilities that your character may learn.

In the box inside the Entanglement meter write down the sum of your Willpower + Guts + 3. The number indicates your starting amount of Entanglement, using it will cause the meter to drop lower and certain circumstances may cause the meter to go above this limit.

Indicate the number you wrote down in the box by drawing a line after that spot on the meter. Later, when you gain or use entanglement you can use a light pencil mark or a paperclip to indicate your current number of entanglement points.

Body

This section is in the right column on your sheet. It shows how much damage your character can take in a fight.

In the section labeled Wound Max, write down the sum of your Stamina + Toughness + 3. This number indicates the maximum amount of damage you can take to any body part.

Indicate the number you wrote down in the box by drawing a line after that spot on the meter for each body part. Later, when you get injured or heal you can use a light pencil mark to indicate the current number of wounds on that location.

Defenses

This section is at the top of the combat column on the right half of your sheet. It shows your character's ability to avoid getting hurt in a fight.

In the box labeled Passive Defense, write down the number of points you have in the skill Defense. If this number is 0 write 1 instead. This is the target number for an enemy to hit you when you are not actively dodging.

In the box labeled Melee Dodge, write down your roll for Agility + Defense This is what you roll whenever you actively try to avoid a close combat attack.

In the box labeled Ranged Dodge, write down your roll for Wits + Defense. This is what you roll whenever you actively try to avoid a ranged attack.

Attacks

Decide with the GM what weapons you will start with and write down the name of each on the first line of a slot (most characters can start with a basic weapon if it makes sense for their character concept, if you put points in the skill Resources you can potentially start with more). You can fill in as many or as few attacks as you like . It is a good idea to write to the rolls for unarmed attacks in one of the slots.

For each attack, write the attack roll (Finesse + Melee for melee attacks, Perception + Firearms for firearms, or Perception + Ballistics for other ranged attacks, or Strength + Unarmed for unarmed attacks) in the box in the first row.

Each attack will also have boxes labeled Damage, Speed, Ammo, and Range. Fill in each slot with the stats listed in chapter 7 of the rulebook for that weapon. For unarmed attacks the Damage roll is Strength + 0.

Step 6: Finishing Touches

Your character is almost done! If you haven't already written down a character concept do so now. All that is left now is to flesh out any details that define your character more. It's not necessary to add any extra details to your character, but the more you do the more interesting they will be to play.

If you are using the app you can hit the finish button and load up your new character. You can write any additional details you wish in any of the Notes sections at the bottom of every section of the character sheet.

Think of any back story, personality traits, quirks, or any other details that would make your character more interesting and fun to roleplay. Feel free to write any of these traits in the notes section(s) of your character sheet or just remember them.

Also, you can write down any possessions your character has in the equipment section. The skill Resources influences how much your character can own and how nice things are, decide with you GM what you should write down. You only need to write down the specific equipment that your character is going to carry around with him or specific important items. Mundane things like clothes or household items can be assumed without writing them down. The amount of points you have in the Resources skill determines how much stuff your character can be assumed to own.

Skill Descriptions

Alertness - Perception
Ability to notice things and avoid surprise.

Athletics - Agility
Ability to excel in physical activities, overcome obstacles, and move quickly and skillfully.
Every even point of Athletics aises your Speed and every odd point raises your Endurance.

Charm - Charisma
Ability to befriend, persuade, gain the favor of others, and get your way in social situations.

Drive - Finesse
Skill at operating vehicles.

Guts - Willpower
Strength of personality and ability to resist mental influence and fear.
Each point raises your Entanglement.

Hardware- Intelligence
Ability to make and repair objects, machines, and computer hardware as well as utilize tools and vocational skills.

Humanities- Intelligence
Education, knowledge of the humanities, skill with written communication, and the ability to fit in academia. Covers the arts, history, social sciences, law, culture, and ability to research, study, and find and digest information.

Investigate - Perception
Ability to search, sense motives, find clues, and solve mysteries.

Manipulate - Charisma
Ability to coerce, threaten, deceive, and frighten others to get your way.

Medicine - Intelligence
Knowledge of healing, first aid, anatomy, and using medical equipment.

Science - Intelligence
Knowledge of the hard sciences and ability to research and experiment.

Software - Intelligence
Ability to use, program, and hack computers.

Stealth - Wits
Ability to hide, sneak, move silently, avoid detection, and circumvent security systems.

Survival - Perception
Ability to provide food and shelter, for yourself and others in hostile environments.

Toughness - Strength
Ability to withstand physical stress, disease, and resist damage.
Each point raises your Wound Max.

Combat Skills

Ballistics - Perception
Proficiency with throwing weapons, archery, and grenade launchers. Also improves the ability to aim difficult shots with regular firearms.

Defense - Agility in melee combat, Wits in Ranged Combat
Ability to block or dodge attacks.

Firearms - Perception
Proficiency with handguns, shotguns, and rifles.

Initiative - Wits
Reflexes and reaction times. Determines the order of combat.

Melee - Finesse
Skill in armed hand to hand combat with knives, swords, clubs, and other melee weapons.

Unarmed - Finesse
Ability in unarmed combat including grappling, disarming, and tripping.

Unrolled Skills

These skills are not associated with an attribute and typically aren't rolled. With the exception of Languages they cannot be improved by styles but they may be modified as the consequences of your roleplaying and events in the game's narrative.

Connections
A measure of social standing, fame, contacts, and friends in high places at the start of the game. When choosing this skill discuss with your GM what specific connections this skill represents, it may represent your perks of your occupation or specific people you know. Ranks in this skill may represent either increased geographic area of your influence or increased utility of your connections in a small area. The consequences of in-game actions may increase or decrease this stat.

0 = You have little influence on society.
1 = Local. You are a local celebrity or have contacts with access to useful information.
2 = Regional. You either known across a few states or widely in a specific subculture, have contacts with influential positions, or are close to a single very useful person.
3 = National. You are either nationally famous, have friends in very high places, or have access to a single extremely powerful person.

Languages
This skill increases the number of languages you are fluent and literate in. Choose these languages and write them in your character notes at character creation or when investing in this skill.

0 = You know only your native tongue.
1 = You know your native tongue and 1 additional language (bilingual).
2 = You 4 total languages.
3 = You 6 total languages.

Resources
A measure of wealth and possessions at the start of the game. Your character concept will influence what this skill represents, choose what it means with your GM when creating this character, it may mean an occupation, inherited wealth, or specific equipment. Generally, the more points invested here, the better equipment a character can start with. The consequences of in-game actions may increase or decrease this stat.

0 = Your needs are met, but you have little additional purchasing power.
1 = Comfortable. You can afford a nice residence and vehicle.
2 = Millionaire. You could afford multiple residences.
3 = Billionaire. Most expenses are trivial to you.
